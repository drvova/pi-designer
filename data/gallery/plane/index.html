Cover Feature

The Vertical City

As urban populations swell and horizontal land grows scarce, architects are reimagining the high-rise not as a tower of isolation but as a vertical neighbourhood — intricate, layered, and unmistakably public.

By Eleanor Voss  ·  Photography by Markus Chen

The skyline of every major city tells a story of ambition — steel reaching upward as land values climb. But a new generation of architects argues that tall buildings have failed their social promise. Stacking identical floor plates on a central core produces efficient real estate, not community. The result, they say, is a city of isolated towers connected only by wind and shadow.

In response, firms across Europe and Asia are prototyping what might be called the "vertical neighbourhood" — a high-rise whose floors are not carbon copies but differentiated spaces that blend living, working, and public life within a single structure. The key insight is that density and sociability are not opposites when the architecture mediates between them.

Projects such as MVRDV's Valley in Amsterdam and the mixed-use towers of Singapore's Marina Bay demonstrate that variation in floor plate, sky gardens at multiple elevations, and genuinely public programme at grade can transform the high-rise from a private enclave into an urban contributor.

In Depth

Reimagining the Public Realm

By Daniel Auerbach · Photographs by Ingrid Solvik · 22 min read

Apublic square is never empty. Even when no one occupies its benches or crosses its paving, the square holds the memory of assembly, the geometry of civic intention, and the silent argument of who belongs and who does not. For two decades, European cities have poured investment into public space — pedestrianising thoroughfares, installing paving patterns and permanent furniture, commissioning landmark pavilions. Yet the most honest question facing urban designers today is whether these spaces are genuinely public or merely publicly accessible.

The distinction matters. A space can be open to everyone in law while remaining unwelcoming in fact — through sight lines that favour surveillance over comfort, seating that repels rest, programme that excludes without a ticket. The term "public realm" has become a developer's amenity, as negotiable as a gym membership or a concierge desk. True public space, by contrast, is defined not by ownership but by use: it is the ground where strangers negotiate coexistence without a script.

Recent projects suggest a quiet return to first principles. In Barcelona, the transformation of the Via Laietana is less about new architecture than about redistributing existing space — widening pavements, reducing vehicle lanes, introducing permeable surfaces that allow the soil beneath to breathe. The result is not photogenic, which may be its greatest virtue. It does not declare itself as a destination; it simply makes the everyday city more habitable.

Similarly, the ongoing work at Munich's Karlsplatz has rejected the temptation to install a signature building. Instead, the design team has focused on the ground plane: lowering road levels to create a continuous pedestrian surface, introducing staggered granite blocks that double as seating and drainage, and planting a grove of honey locusts whose canopy will, in a decade, define the space more than any wall. The architecture here is almost invisible — and that is the point.

"True public space is defined not by ownership but by use: the ground where strangers negotiate coexistence without a script." — Daniel Auerbach

The most contested frontier of public space may be the street itself. Paris, under its "Ville du Quart d'Heure" initiative, has begun reclassifying road space not as a transportation corridor but as a sequence of public rooms — each intersection a potential square, each widened pavement a potential front porch. The architectural implication is subtle but profound: when the street becomes a room, the buildings that line it become its walls, and their ground-floor facades must perform as interior surfaces, not as monuments to corporate identity.

What these projects share is a refusal to monumentalise. They understand that public space is a practice, not a product. The architect's role is not to deliver a finished composition but to construct the conditions under which public life can emerge — a ground plane with enough ambiguity to accommodate the unpredictable. This demands a kind of humility that the profession does not always reward. The next great public space may well be one that no one photographs on opening day, because it looks unfinished. A decade later, it will be indispensable.

The lessons for architects working at any scale are clear: resist the urge to over-determine. Design the edges, the surfaces, the transitions — the moments where public life actually happens — and let the centre remain open. The most powerful civic gesture is sometimes simply to step back and leave room for someone else to act.

Reviews
Book

The Architecture of Absence

By Marina Otero Verzier — MIT Press, 2026

Otero Verzier constructs a rigorous argument for what she calls "negative architectural practice" — design that operates through removal, subtraction, and deliberate incompleteness. Drawing on case studies from Detroit to Detmold, the book challenges the profession's addiction to addition without ever lapsing into cynicism. Essential reading for anyone who suspects that the most ethical architectural act may sometimes be to build nothing at all.

Reviewed by Alice Durand
Exhibition

Concrete Utopias

Museum of Modern Art, Oslo — until September 2026

A survey of Scandinavian brutalism that resists the easy nostalgia of recent revivals. Curator Hanna Eide has assembled a sharp selection of original drawings, period photography, and newly commissioned documentation that foregrounds the social ambitions behind the raw concrete: universal healthcare, public education, collective housing. The buildings are not always beautiful, but the ideals that shaped them deserve this careful, unsentimental reckoning.

Reviewed by Tomás Guerra
