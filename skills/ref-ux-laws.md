﻿25 Design Concepts to Learn
By @teagan_talks on IG
* Aesthetic-Usability Effect
People tend to perceive visually appealing designs as easier to use, even if they aren’t actually more functional.
* Peak-End Rule
Users evaluate a product based on the emotional high point and the final interaction.
* Jakob’s Law
Users expect your product to work like other products they already know and prefer familiar patterns over new ones.
* Miller’s Law
The average person can only hold about 7 items (plus or minus 2) in their working memory at once.
* Occam’s Razor
The simplest solution is usually the best, so unnecessary complexity should be removed.
   * Hick’s Law
The more choices you give users, the longer it takes them to make a decision.
   * Fitts’s Law
The bigger and closer a button is, the easier and faster it is to click.
   * Law of Proximity
Objects placed near each other are perceived as related.
   * Law of Similarity
Elements that look alike are assumed to have the same function.
   * Law of Common Region
Items within the same boundary are seen as grouped together.
      * Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)
Items that stand out visually are more likely to be remembered.
      * Serial Position Effect
People remember the first and last items in a sequence best.
      * Doherty Threshold
Users feel most engaged when systems respond within about 400 milliseconds.
      * Zeigarnik Effect
People remember unfinished tasks better than completed ones.
         * Progressive Disclosure
Show only the necessary information first, revealing more as needed.
         * Law of Prägnanz
People interpret complex visuals in the simplest way possible.
         * Selective Attention
Users focus only on what feels relevant and ignore everything else.
         * Recognition Over Recall
It’s easier for users to recognize options than remember them from scratch.
         * Chunking
Breaking information into smaller groups makes it easier to process.
            * Feedback Loop
Users need immediate feedback after actions to understand what happened.
            * Affordance
Design elements should suggest how they are meant to be used.
            * Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)
Most results come from a small portion of inputs, so focus on what matters most.
            * Aesthetic Integrity
The design should match the purpose and tone of the product.
            * Postel’s Law
Be flexible in what you accept from users, but strict in what you output.
